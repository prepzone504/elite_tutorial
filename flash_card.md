1. Which vessel carries oxygenated blood to the heart muscle?
ANSWER: Coronary arteries. While the heart pumps oxygenated blood to the rest of the body, it cannot absorb oxygen directly from the blood inside its chambers. Instead, it relies on its own set of blood vessels—the coronary arteries. These arteries branch off from the aorta and supply the heart muscle (myocardium) with the oxygen and nutrients it needs to keep pumping.

2. Which chamber has the thickest wall?
ANSWER: The left ventricle. The heart has four chambers, but the left ventricle has the thickest muscular wall. This is because it must pump oxygenated blood through the aorta and out to the entire body. It needs a massive amount of force to overcome the high resistance of the systemic circulation, unlike the right ventricle which only needs to pump blood a short distance to the lungs.

3. Foramen ovale in fetal circulation allows blood to:
ANSWER: Bypass the lungs by flowing directly from the right atrium to the left atrium. In a fetus, the lungs are not yet functioning for gas exchange (the mother's placenta handles this). The foramen ovale is a small hole between the upper two chambers of the heart that allows oxygen-rich blood to skip the pulmonary (lung) circuit and be pumped straight out to the developing body.

4. T wave inversion on ECG suggests:
ANSWER: Myocardial ischemia. An electrocardiogram (ECG) records the electrical activity of the heart. The T wave represents ventricular repolarization (the heart muscle resetting after a beat). When the T wave is inverted (flipped upside down), it is a classic sign of myocardial ischemia—meaning the heart muscle is not getting enough oxygen, often due to narrowed coronary arteries.

5. Which is NOT a determinant of stroke volume?
ANSWER: Heart rate. Stroke volume is the amount of blood pumped out of the heart with each beat. It is determined by three main factors: Preload (the stretch of the heart muscle before it contracts), Afterload (the resistance the heart must pump against), and Contractility (the inherent strength of the heart muscle). Heart rate affects overall cardiac output, but does not directly determine the stroke volume itself.

6. Which valve disorder causes wide pulse pressure?
ANSWER: Aortic regurgitation. Pulse pressure is the difference between your systolic (top) and diastolic (bottom) blood pressure numbers. In aortic regurgitation, the aortic valve doesn't close properly. Blood leaks back into the left ventricle during diastole, causing the diastolic pressure to drop significantly. Meanwhile, the heart pumps harder to compensate, raising the systolic pressure. This large gap creates a "wide" pulse pressure.

7. Which enzyme rises first after MI?
ANSWER: Myoglobin. When heart muscle cells are damaged during a myocardial infarction (MI or heart attack), they release specific proteins into the blood. Myoglobin is the first to rise, typically within 1-3 hours of injury. However, because it is found in all muscle tissue (not just the heart), it is not specific to heart damage. Troponin is the preferred biomarker because it is highly specific to cardiac muscle, even though it rises slightly later.

8. Normal potassium range:
ANSWER: 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L. Potassium is a crucial electrolyte that regulates the electrical impulses of the heart. Even minor deviations outside this narrow range can be extremely dangerous. Hypokalemia (low potassium) can cause lethal arrhythmias and increase the risk of digoxin toxicity, while hyperkalemia (high potassium) can lead to cardiac arrest.

9. Absolute contraindication to stress test:
ANSWER: Unstable angina. A stress test involves making the heart work harder (via exercise or medication) to see if it gets enough blood flow under pressure. Unstable angina means the patient is experiencing unpredictable chest pain even at rest, indicating severe and unstable blockages in the coronary arteries. Putting this heart under deliberate stress could easily trigger a full-blown heart attack.

10. Gold standard to diagnose DVT:
ANSWER: Venous duplex ultrasound. Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is a blood clot in a deep vein, usually in the leg. Venous duplex ultrasound is the most reliable, non-invasive way to diagnose it. It uses sound waves to visualize the veins and measure the speed and direction of blood flow, allowing doctors to clearly see if a clot is blocking the vessel.

11. Which test diagnoses congenital heart defects best?
ANSWER: Echocardiogram. Congenital heart defects are structural problems present at birth (like holes in the heart or abnormal valves). An echocardiogram is an ultrasound of the heart that provides real-time, moving images of the heart's chambers, valves, and blood flow. It is the gold standard because it safely and accurately visualizes these structural abnormalities without radiation.

12. Cardiac enzymes: CK-MB peaks at:
ANSWER: 12 to 24 hours. Creatine kinase-MB (CK-MB) is an enzyme found primarily in heart muscle cells. After a heart attack, CK-MB levels begin to rise in the blood within 3 to 6 hours, reach their absolute highest point (peak) at 12 to 24 hours, and generally return to normal within 2 to 3 days.

13. Which is highest risk factor for CAD?
ANSWER: Diabetes mellitus. Coronary artery disease (CAD) has many risk factors, but diabetes is considered a "CAD equivalent." This means a person with diabetes has the same high risk of having a heart attack as someone who has already had one. Chronic high blood sugar damages the inner lining of blood vessels, accelerating the buildup of plaque (atherosclerosis).

14. Before giving metoprolol, hold for:
ANSWER: Heart rate less than 60 bpm. Metoprolol is a beta-blocker. It works by blocking the effects of adrenaline on the heart, causing it to beat more slowly and with less force. This lowers blood pressure and reduces the heart's workload. However, if the patient's resting heart rate is already too low (bradycardia, typically below 60), giving metoprolol could drop their heart rate to dangerously low levels.

15. Antidote for heparin:
ANSWER: Protamine sulfate. Heparin is a powerful anticoagulant (blood thinner) used to prevent and treat clots. If a patient receives too much heparin or starts bleeding excessively, protamine sulfate is administered as an emergency reversal agent. It chemically binds to heparin, neutralizing its blood-thinning effects immediately.

16. Which electrolyte increases digoxin toxicity?
ANSWER: Hypokalemia (low potassium). Digoxin is a medication used for heart failure and irregular heartbeats. It works by binding to the same receptor sites on the heart muscle cells that potassium normally binds to. If potassium levels are too low, there is less competition, allowing digoxin to bind excessively. This easily leads to digoxin toxicity, which can cause severe visual disturbances (halos) and fatal arrhythmias.

17. Clopidogrel is a:
ANSWER: Antiplatelet medication. While often lumped in with "blood thinners," clopidogrel specifically stops platelets (tiny blood cells) from sticking together and forming clots. It is fundamentally different from anticoagulants (like warfarin or heparin) which target blood clotting proteins. Clopidogrel is crucial for patients with stents to prevent clots from suddenly blocking the newly opened artery.

18. ECG in pericarditis shows:
ANSWER: ST elevation in all leads. Pericarditis is the inflammation of the sac surrounding the heart. Because the inflammation is widespread across the entire surface of the heart, an ECG will show ST-segment elevation in almost all leads. This is a key difference from a heart attack (MI), where ST elevation only appears in the specific leads looking at the localized damaged area of the heart.

19. Patient with HF teaching. Which indicates need for further teaching?
ANSWER: “I take extra furosemide if I’m short of breath.” Heart failure (HF) patients are often prescribed furosemide (a diuretic) to eliminate excess fluid buildup. While shortness of breath can indicate fluid in the lungs, patients should never adjust their diuretic doses independently. Taking too much can cause severe dehydration, kidney injury, and dangerous electrolyte imbalances like hypokalemia.

20. Patient with chest pain. Which order to do FIRST?
ANSWER: Oxygen if SpO2 < 90%. When a patient presents with sudden chest pain (a potential heart attack), the priority is preserving the dying heart muscle. The heart is starving for oxygen. Providing supplemental oxygen (if their oxygen saturation is low) is the most immediate way to improve oxygen delivery to the struggling tissues before moving on to ECGs, nitroglycerin, or blood work.

21. The posterior communicating artery connects the:
ANSWER: Internal carotid and posterior cerebral arteries. This artery is a crucial part of the Circle of Willis, a ring-like vascular structure at the base of the brain. By connecting the front circulation (internal carotid) with the back circulation (posterior cerebral), it acts as a backup system. If one major artery gets blocked, blood can reroute through the communicating arteries to keep the brain supplied with oxygen.

22. Prolonged occlusion of the right coronary artery produces an infarction in which of the following areas of the heart?
ANSWER: The inferior area of the heart. The right coronary artery (RCA) wraps around the right side and bottom (inferior) portion of the heart, supplying blood to the right ventricle and the bottom wall of the left ventricle. A blockage here causes an "inferior wall MI." Because the RCA also supplies the SA and AV nodes (the heart's pacemakers), these patients often experience dangerous drops in heart rate.

23. When teaching a patient why spironolactone (Aldactone) and furosemide (Lasix) are prescribed together, the nurse bases teaching on the knowledge that:
ANSWER: This combination promotes fluid loss while preventing hypokalemia. Furosemide is a powerful "loop diuretic" that forces the kidneys to excrete fluid, but it dangerously washes out potassium in the process. Spironolactone is a weaker diuretic but is "potassium-sparing." Prescribing them together balances the electrolytes—furosemide removes the fluid, and spironolactone holds onto the potassium.

24. To evaluate a client’s condition following cardiac catheterization, the nurse will palpate the pulse:
ANSWER: Distal to the catheter insertion site. Cardiac catheterization usually involves inserting a large tube into the femoral artery (groin) or radial artery (wrist). The biggest risk after the procedure is a blood clot forming at the puncture site and blocking blood flow to the rest of the limb. Checking the pulse below (distal to) the site ensures blood is still successfully making its way down the arm or leg.

25. Direct-acting vasodilators have which of the following effects on the heart rate?
ANSWER: Heart rate increases (reflex tachycardia). Direct-acting vasodilators rapidly relax the smooth muscle in blood vessels, causing blood pressure to drop sharply. The body senses this sudden drop and panics. The nervous system instantly compensates by releasing adrenaline to speed up the heart rate (tachycardia) in a desperate attempt to bring the blood pressure back up to normal.

26. A 45-year-old male client with leg ulcers and arterial insufficiency is admitted to the hospital. The nurse understands that leg ulcers of this nature are usually caused by:
ANSWER: Atherosclerotic obstruction of the arteries. Arterial insufficiency means fresh, oxygen-rich blood is struggling to reach the legs due to plaque buildup (atherosclerosis) narrowing the arteries. Without adequate oxygen and nutrients, the tissue literally begins to starve and die, leading to painful, hard-to-heal arterial ulcers, typically found on the toes, heels, or ankles.

27. In which of the following types of cardiomyopathy does cardiac output remain normal?
ANSWER: Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. In this condition, the heart muscle (especially the left ventricle) becomes abnormally thick. Despite the thickening, the heart's squeezing power (contractility) remains strong, so it can still pump out a normal volume of blood at rest. The main issue is that the thick walls make the chamber stiff, preventing it from relaxing and filling properly between beats.

28. A patient with chest pain is most likely experiencing MI if pain:
ANSWER: Radiates to the jaw and left arm. Referred pain occurs when pain signals from the heart travel along the same nerve pathways that connect to the arm, neck, and jaw. While heartburn or muscle strains stay localized, chest pain that travels outward—especially accompanied by sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath—is a massive red flag for a myocardial infarction.

29. Early sign of shock is:
ANSWER: Tachycardia (increased heart rate). Shock occurs when the body's tissues are not getting enough blood and oxygen. Long before blood pressure starts to drop (which is a late and very dangerous sign), the body's first defense mechanism is to pump the heart faster. Tachycardia is the body's alarm system trying to circulate whatever blood is available as quickly as possible.

30. A client comes into the E.R. with acute shortness of breath and a cough that produces pink, frothy sputum. Admission assessment reveals crackles and wheezes, a BP of 85/46, a HR of 122 BPM, and a respiratory rate of 38 breaths/minute. Which of the following disorders should the nurse suspect?
ANSWER: Pulmonary edema. This is a life-threatening emergency usually caused by left-sided heart failure. The left ventricle fails to pump blood forward, causing pressure to back up into the lungs. This immense pressure forces fluid and microscopic amounts of blood out of the lung capillaries and into the air sacs (alveoli). When the patient breathes through this fluid, it creates a bubbly, pink, frothy sputum.
